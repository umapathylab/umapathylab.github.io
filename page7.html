Lion-Tailed Macaque Project

Understanding the extinction process of mammalian species in Human-dominated landscapes



Habitat fragmentation is the most serious threat to biological diversity and is the primary cause of the present extinction crisis. The Western Ghats, especially tropical rain forests, are well known as a centre of very high biodiversity. Nearly a century of human activities in this area has removed vast areas of forests. The remaining rain forest occurs as a number of isolated small forest fragments. Studies in the last twenty years have revealed that substantial changes have occurred in the animal communities in rainforest fragments in the Western Ghats, with the endemics being adversely affected (Umapathy and Kumar 2000). One of the most adversely affected species is the lion-tailed macaque, and it is also one of the most endangered primates of India. It is an ideal model to examine the factors that influence the survival of small populations isolated in forest fragments. Many of these small populations have survived in the habitat fragments for more than 50 years (Umapathy and Kumar 2000a). However, birth/growth rate and proportion of immatures are considerably lower than in the large populations, indicating long-term population decline (Umapathy & Kumar 2000a; Umapathy et al. 2011, Laurance et al. 2012). Ecological factors such as fragment area and food availability seem to only partly explain this long-term population decline (Umapathy and Kumar 2000b) and these isolated populations have also lost their genetic variation and are facing the threat of local extinction (Ram et al. 2015). Over and above these endemics were severely infected with non-native parasites due to high anthropogenic activities (Hussain et al. 2013; Chakraborty et al 2015). Further studies were initiated to understand the micro and macro climate changes on the survival these endangered animals in the fragmented landscape.

Of late many, protected areas are becoming popular destinations for vacations, and surprisingly few information is available on tourism impacts on wildlife. Tourism can have an adverse impact on three ways by causing changes in behavior, physiology and cause damage to the natural habitats (Bhattacharjee et al. 2015). Wild animals are often vulnerable to anthropogenic disturbance especially during breeding. Any disturbance during courtship and mating can directly affect reproductive success of species. With a fast increase in tourism, it is important to determine the impacts which are occurring despite the difficulty of quantifying these changes. Our group aimed to understand the anthropogenic impacts on the reproductive function in larger mammals of the large protected areas.

Once the wild population becomes very low and faces the danger of extinction, the only way to save such animals is to bring them into captivity, breed them and increase their numbers to re-establish the wild population (Umapathy 2007). Breeding of endangered species involves many challenges, both biological and political and it helps by exercising control over breeding to avoid inbreeding and to the increase in the reproductive rate. We have successfully demonstrated by breeding mouse deer from 6 to 128 individuals with the collaboration of Nehru Zoological Park, Hyderabad and Central Zoo Authority of India (Parvathi et al. 2014). There are instances when because of reasons such as incompatibility, infertility or inability, particular animals do not breed in captive conditions. In such cases, when the numbers of animals are very low, artificial methods to assist breeding for increasing the population may be the only option, to increase the chances of survival of particular animals (Umapathy et al. 2007; Sontakke et al 2009).
